Key-phrase: Common Core

The Common Core was developed in 2009 by the National Governors Association (NGA) and the Council of Cheif State School Officers (CCSSO), and was finally published in June of 2010 [1]. The CCSSO is a non-partisan polical organization that consists of public officals who head deparments of elementary and secondary education in the United States. The NGA is similarly a non-paritsan public policy organization that is made up of governors from US states, territories, and commonwealths [3]. By 2011 individual states had begun the process of reviewing, adopting, and ratifying the adoption of the Common Core State Standards, and as of 2015 42 states have adopted the CCSS in English Language Arts (ELA)/literacy and mathematics [1].

Through the Tenth Amendment to the US Constitution, the roles of education and curriculum development are largely left up to individual States, and thus the Common Core was developed to provide consistent educational standards across states, and to ensure that students who graduate high school are prepared for college programs and to enter the workforce [4][5]. The standards were developed within two different categories: (1) standards to assess readiness for college education and entering the workforce, (2) educational expectations in ELA/literacy and math at the conclusion of each year of education from grades K-12 [1]. While the Common Core Standards specify what students should know at the end of each grade, individual school districts are still resopnsible for choosing curricula based upon the standards [5].
